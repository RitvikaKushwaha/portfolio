Research Interests

Rise of Stretchable Hydrogels

Hydrogel is receiving great attention due to its various applications in the field of wound healing, drug delivery, tissue engineering, sensing, etc. It is a three-dimensional polymer network having the ability to preserve water in its porous structure. There are majorly two kinds of hydrogel based on their molecular structure- covalently cross-linked hydrogel and physically cross-linked hydrogel such as electrostatic interaction, ùúã ‚àí ùúã interaction, hydrophobic interaction, hydrogen bonding, host-guest interaction, etc. Its capacity to hold water arises due to the presence of supramolecular interaction in it due to the presence of hydrophilic groups such as amino, carboxyl, and hydroxyl groups. However, most traditional hydrogels suffer from lower mechanical strength, so they cannot withstand high stress or strain, which highly limits their applications. Therefore, the preparation of a hydrogel with high water content, high mechanical strength, and fatigue resistance still remains a challenge. To overcome this limitation, Gong et al. in 2003 first discovered a double network (DN) highly stretchable hydrogel. In general, stretchable hydrogels are made from at least two polymer chains cross-linked chemically through dynamic or mutually exchangeable covalent bonds and physically through non-covalent interactions such as hydrogen bond, ùúã ‚àí ùúã interaction, hydrophobic interaction, and ionic interactions, freeze-thaw cycles, etc. Under deformation forces, these dynamic covalent or supramolecular crosslinks provide stretchability to these hydrogels through redistribution within the hydrogel network. Hence, stretchable hydrogels can be realized through rationalized design and the selection of these interacting crosslinkers. Although, according to reports two or more networks are required for stretchable hydrogel formation but there are few reports as well that state that even using one polymeric network highly stretchable hydrogel can be engineered.
